# Jeremy Haynes Scaling Systems: Forester Content Ads, Offer Math, Proof Flywheel, Ascension Path

Source: four Jeremy Haynes (Megalodon Marketing) skill bundles, read in full: content-ads-cold-traffic (the Forester), offer-math-pricing, offer-proof-flywheel, upsell-ascension-path. These are the SCALE layer: what happens after the offer and funnel work.

## 1. The Forester: Content Ads That Make Cold Traffic Buy at Scale

Core mechanism: platforms price impressions by audience temperature. Getting someone to watch just 3 seconds of ANY content piece costs 1 to 2 pennies and manufactures warm-audience status. DR ads aimed at that manufactured warm audience get dramatically lower CPMs (documented drops from $200 to 300 CPMs down to double digits) plus better show rates, close rates, shorter sales cycles, higher AOV.

Structure: two Content Cycle Bins plus DR, all launched simultaneously.

- Cycle Bin 1 (cold views): dozens of content pieces, one campaign, ONE AD SET PER PIECE, video views objective, cold targeting. Per-piece 3-second view exclusion so nobody sees the same piece twice. This creates the swarm effect: they keep seeing NEW content from you, manufacturing familiarity bias. Master retargeting audience = watched 3 seconds of any Bin 1 video.
- Cycle Bin 2 (strategic content): testimonials, case studies, stage talks, process explainers, objection-handling content. Targets the manufactured warm audience. NO CTAs anywhere in cycle bins: content builds trust, DR closes.
- DR campaign: your normal funnel ads (call, webinar, VSL, DM), targeting the same manufactured warm audience, running CONCURRENTLY with Bin 2, not after.

Key numbers and rules:
- Volume play: minimum 10 pieces, ideal 20 to 50+. Under 10 pieces, don't run it.
- Content-to-audience ratio: at least 1 piece per 25,000 people in the target audience.
- Audience retention window by sales cycle: under 14 days = 30-day window, 14 to 60 days = 60-day, 60+ days = 90-day, never past 180.
- Budget split: Bin 1 gets the largest share (roughly 45 to 50%), Bin 2 and DR split the rest.
- 3-second exclusions are non-negotiable. Without them the swarm breaks.
- Attribution is blended: content spend + DR spend = blended ROAS. Prove lift with a 2 to 4 week split test vs DR-only, ignoring the first 5 to 7 ramp-up days.
- Refresh content every 2 to 4 weeks. Frequency above 3 to 4x per week on a piece means it's exhausting.
- When NOT to use: a sales team that closes cold at high rates (skip straight to DR), or an audience needing deep sequential education (use a sequential 3-video funnel with view-percentage gates instead).

## 2. Offer Math: Raising Prices With Data, Not Guesswork

Core insight: most offers are more INELASTIC than their operators assume. Fear, not data, keeps businesses underpriced. If a 20% price raise loses only 5% of customers, revenue jumps.

Elasticity: (% change in quantity) / (% change in price). Under 1 = inelastic = raise. Proxy signals for inelastic when there's no historical data: high close rates, long tenure, few equivalent competitors, customers who refer.

Diagnostic reads:
- "Nobody complains about price" = underpriced. Real value doesn't produce zero resistance.
- Close rate above 80% = price too low. Some resistance is healthy, it means you're near the value ceiling.
- "We priced off competitors" = anchored to someone else's cost structure, and they're probably underpriced too.
- "I'd never pay that much" = irrelevant. You are not your customer.

Value-based pricing, 3 steps: (A) name the OUTCOME customers actually buy, not the deliverable (not "coaching" but "going from $300K to $1M"). The outcome value sets the ceiling, delivery cost sets the floor, most price at the floor. (B) Map alternatives: competitors, DIY, doing nothing, adjacent solutions. If the best alternative costs more or delivers less, you have pricing power. (C) Price at 10 to 20% of the 12-month value delivered. A service generating $500K justifies $50K to $100K.

Tier structure (good / better / best): Good at 40 to 60% of mid, captures walkers. Better at the value-based price, the workhorse where most buyers should land. Best at 1.5 to 2.5x mid, exists mainly to ANCHOR the mid-tier while catching premium buyers, and must contain things customers genuinely want, not filler.

Testing rules: A/B on NEW customers only (they have no anchor), 50 to 100 conversions per group. Grandfather existing customers, the goodwill often pays back more in retention and referrals than the forgone raise. Van Westendorp 4-question survey (too cheap / bargain / getting expensive / too expensive) plots the acceptable range and optimal point.

CLV is the deciding metric, not first-transaction revenue: CLV = (avg purchase value x frequency x lifespan) minus CAC. Model current vs new pricing with projected volume drop. Higher-priced customers also stay longer, refer more, and need less support.

## 3. The Offer Proof Flywheel: Wins Create Proof Creates Wins

Four stages that loop: (1) design offers to generate measurable wins, (2) systematically capture wins, (3) showcase proof where decisions happen, (4) iterate the offer from win data. Each rotation compounds. The flywheel cannot be faked: if the offer doesn't produce real results, fix the offer first.

Proof strength ranking: quantified results ("revenue up 340% in 90 days") > video testimonials and detailed case studies > screenshots from real platforms > aggregate operational data ("2,117 clients, 0.57% refund rate") > written testimonials > generic praise (worthless).

Design offers FOR wins: clear before-and-after metrics, milestone checkpoints, a natural win moment. "8-week program" becomes "8-week program with Week 2 baseline, Week 4 checkpoint, Week 8 results documentation": same offer, three built-in proof collection moments. Starting from zero proof: run a pilot (limited spots, reduced price, in exchange for documentation participation). The pilot's goal is proof inventory, not revenue.

Capture system rules: automatic (milestone-triggered, never memory-dependent), specific ("what was revenue before and after?" not "how was your experience?"), low-friction, and organized BY CUSTOMER TYPE so prospects can find proof from someone like THEM. Ask for the video AFTER a documented win, with a specific 60-second brief: where you were, what result, what surprised you. Raw phone footage beats polished production.

Showcase strategy: a testimonials page is where proof goes to die. Proof belongs at every decision point: homepage leads with wins not company history, sales pages carry case studies matched to the offer, emails carry proof matched to the prospect's segment, social carries raw transformation stories, sales calls carry "someone in your exact situation" results, ads use customer results as hooks. Plus AI visibility: structured data markup and detailed specific case studies published on-site, because AI overviews and zero-click search favor verifiable specifics.

Iteration loop (what makes it a flywheel): monthly review of wins. Which segments win biggest (target them), which outcomes surprise (make them core offer components when 3+ customers highlight the same one), what patterns actually drive results.

Common killers: waiting for perfect testimonials instead of deploying screenshots today, generic asks, proof museum pages, stale 3-year-old proof, uncategorized proof, using proof without written permission, inflated claims (one verified modest result beats an unverifiable blockbuster).

## 4. The Upsell and Ascension Path: Profit Without New Leads

Core principle: selling more to someone who already bought clears zero trust barrier and costs zero acquisition. If AOV equals LTV, that IS the problem.

The value ladder (3 to 5 rungs, no more):
1. Entry: low-ticket door-opener.
2. Order bump: checkout checkbox add-on at 10 to 30% of the primary price. Must organically enhance the main purchase (templates, audit, resource library). The single easiest revenue win in existence.
3. One-time offer (OTO): thank-you page upgrade riding post-purchase momentum. The bump ENHANCES the purchase, the OTO ELEVATES it (group coaching, fast-track implementation). Time-limited framing, honestly.
4. Core offer: the flagship.
5. Premium: DFY, VIP, mastermind, agency relationship, for the big dogs.

The 30-90 day post-purchase sequence:
- Days 1 to 7 (quick wins): prove competence immediately, kill buyer's remorse. Daily emails first 3 to 5 days.
- Days 8 to 30 (deepening): case studies, advanced tips, community. INTRODUCE the next tier without selling it: "here's what X achieved with this, and then they took it further with Y."
- Days 31 to 60 (ascension window): the explicit sales sequence, 5 to 7 emails, works because 30 days of value preceded it. For high-value customers add PERSONAL outreach: a call or video message converts the big jumps better than any sequence.
- Days 61 to 90 (nurture): never abandon non-ascenders. They remain valuable, may ascend later, and refer.

Segmentation: high engagers get the fast track (don't make ready buyers wait 60 days). Low engagers get more proof and extended nurture, and diagnose WHY they're not engaging (often onboarding, not product). Disengaged customers are RETENTION candidates, not ascension candidates: re-engage first. Deploy a 4-question survey at day 7 to 14 (goal, biggest challenge, confidence 1 to 10, interest in more hands-on support) and tag accordingly.

Automation: tag-based (purchase tags, engagement tags, stage tags, interest tags) driving 8 to 15 concurrent sequences (welcome, quick wins, engagement, ascension per product, re-engagement, win-back, milestone celebration, cross-sell).

Hard rules: never ascend before delivering value at the current tier (the #1 upsell mistake, destroys trust). Tiers must be GENUINELY differentiated or the ladder is broken. Time-to-ascend of 6 to 12 months signals friction. LTV is the north star.

## Where This Maps in Our Pipeline

- Ads department: the Forester is the content-ad architecture around every client's DR campaigns. The ad-script-system file writes the DR creative, the Forester decides the campaign structure and budget split. Client content libraries get audited into Bin 1 vs Bin 2 during onboarding.
- Offer docs and pricing sections: Offer Math is the pricing methodology. Run the elasticity diagnostic questions during client onboarding, price the DFY offer at 10 to 20% of the 12-month outcome value, and structure good/better/best with the Best tier anchoring. Pairs with hormozi-offers.md (value equation and premium-price virtuous cycle) as the WHY, Offer Math as the HOW-MUCH.
- Proof and case studies: the Proof Flywheel is the client fulfillment SOP. Every client engagement gets milestone checkpoints designed in at kickoff (baseline, mid, results), the three capture templates (milestone check-in, 60-second video ask, screenshot ask), and proof categorized by client type. This also feeds our own agency proof: pilot-program framing for the first clients.
- Funnels and emails: the 30-90 day post-purchase sequence is the template for client email backends. Order bump and OTO design go into every funnel build. The ascension window sequence maps directly onto the emails-and-booking framework.
- Client ascension for the agency itself: the value ladder maps our own path (content > community > DFY at $7k > retainer plus rev share). Personal outreach for the big jumps, never sequence-only.
- Copy QA: proof rules apply everywhere: specific verified numbers only, [PROOF:] placeholders where real proof is pending, permission before publishing any client result.
