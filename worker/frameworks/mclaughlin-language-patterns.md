# Advanced Language Patterns Mastery: Larry McLaughlin (Full Book Distillation)

Source: Advanced Language Patterns Mastery, Larry McLaughlin, Leading Edge Publications, 1992. Read cover to cover. This is the NLP language layer that sits UNDER every framework in this folder. Hormozi gives you the offer, Suby gives you the funnel, Rackham gives you the question sequence. McLaughlin gives you the sentence-level mechanics that make each line land in the reader's nervous system instead of bouncing off their skepticism.

Rule for using this file: these patterns are seasoning, not the meal. The market intelligence and the offer are still the copy source. Use these patterns to phrase what the research already proved. Never use them to fake proof or manufacture claims.

## 1. The Core Premise: Words Build Pictures, So Only Describe What You Want Built

- Every word forces the reader to build an internal representation. They cannot process "don't worry" without first representing worry.
- The unconscious does not process negation. "Don't drop the glass" installs dropping the glass. "Hold the glass tightly" installs holding it.
- Copy rule: describe the wanted state, not the unwanted one, EXCEPT when you deliberately want them to represent the pain (problem sections). Then switch to only-positive language for the solution and CTA sections.
- Order of statements creates direction. "I want it. I can't afford it." ends on the block. "I can't afford it. I want it." ends on the desire. Always end sentences, paragraphs, and sections on the representation you want them left holding.

## 2. Verb Tense Shifts: Put the Problem in the Past, the Result in the Present

The single highest-leverage micro-pattern in the book.

- Formula: "So, up until now, you have [problem in past tense], and now you [resource in present tense]."
- "Up until now you have struggled to get clients. Now you know exactly what to say."
- Words that push things into the past: up until now, in the past, used to, before today, back then, that was the old way.
- Words that pull things into the present and future: now, from this point on, starting today, as you begin, going forward.
- In funnel copy: every description of the prospect's pain should be tensed as their past. Every description of life with the offer should be tensed as present or near future, as if already happening.

## 3. Presupposition Word Banks

Words that smuggle a claim past analysis because the sentence only makes sense if the claim is true.

- Automatic and factual: automatically, second nature, constantly, proven, obviously, naturally, of course.
- Permanence (use for the result): lasting, enduring, permanent, forever, locked in.
- Temporary (use for the problem and for objections): fleeting, fades away, vanishes like a dream, passing, for the moment.
- Awareness verbs (presuppose the claim, question only the noticing): realize, notice, aware, discover, recognize, know. "You may already realize how much easier this makes booking calls" presupposes it makes booking calls easier.
- Ordinals and comparatives presuppose the set: "the first thing you'll notice" presupposes more things follow. "an even faster way" presupposes the current way is fast.
- Time subordinates presuppose the main event: before, after, since, when, while. "Before you claim your spot, read this" presupposes claiming the spot.
- "Only, even, just" presuppose expectations: "You'll only pay $997" presupposes it could cost more.
- Re- verbs and "again, too" presuppose it happened before: rediscover, return to, rebuild.
- Spurious not: "I wonder if you are not already seeing how this fits your business" installs the claim while sounding like doubt.
- Rhetorical and negative questions: "Isn't it time you got paid what you're worth?" cannot be processed without accepting the frame.

## 4. Submodality Language: Space and Time Words That Move the Feeling

- Different words change how big, close, bright, or far away an idea feels. Calibrate to the reader.
- Push the problem away and shrink it: fade away, blow over, belongs to the past, break up, slip away, vanish, become extinct, cast away, close down, dim.
- Pull the result close and grow it: close at hand, just around the corner, lies ahead, look forward, bring to mind, come to light, breakthrough, immense, limitless, crowning.
- Speed words for urgency: at the speed of light, faster, increase the tempo, at once, now or never.
- Copy use: pain paragraphs use shrinking and distancing words on the OLD life, growing and approaching words on the pain itself while agitating. Solution paragraphs reverse it.

## 5. Levels of Abstraction: Chunk Up for Agreement, Chunk Down for Action

- Big picture language gets agreement, specific language gets action. Miscommunication happens when you pitch at the wrong altitude.
- Move to the abstract to get a yes ("every business needs a predictable way to get clients"), then walk down to specifics only as needed ("here is the 3-step system, here is step one").
- Chunk up question: "What is this an example of? For what purpose?" Chunk down: "What specifically? What are the parts?"
- Funnel structure use: headlines and hooks live high (identity and outcome level). Body copy walks down (mechanism, steps, proof). CTA is fully concrete (click, book, time, date).

## 6. Logical Levels: Sell at Identity, Not at Behavior

Levels from bottom to top: Environment (where, when), Behavior (what I do), Capability (what I can), Beliefs and Values (why), Identity (who I am), System (who else).

- A message aimed one or two levels above where the prospect is stuck creates change. "You can't get clients" is a capability complaint. Answer it at belief or identity: "You're the kind of operator who fixes systems, and client flow is just a system."
- Put their limitation in the past AND at a low level. Put the resource in the present AND at identity level: "So you ARE a closer" beats "you closed one deal."
- Avatar docs should note which logical level the market's complaints live at, so copy can aim one level higher.

## 7. The Milton Model: 32 Hypnotic Language Patterns

Artfully vague language the reader completes with their own meaning. One line each, with the copy move.

1. Mind reading: claim to know their thoughts. "You already know referrals alone won't scale."
2. Lost performative: judgment with the judge deleted. "It's been proven that offers with guarantees convert better."
3. Cause and effect: X causes Y. "Watching this training will change how you price forever."
4. Complex equivalent: X means Y. "Booked calls mean freedom."
5. Presuppositions: see section 3.
6. Universal quantifiers: all, every, always, never. "Every coach hits this wall."
7. Modal operators: must, can, should, have to. Match the market's own modal words from research, then chain them (section 11).
8. Nominalizations: process verbs frozen into nouns (growth, freedom, success). Vague on purpose, the reader fills in their version.
9. Unspecified verbs: "This will transform your business" with how unspecified.
10. Tag questions: turn statements into agreements. "That would be useful, wouldn't it?"
11. Lack of referential index: "One can build a 7-figure practice on one offer."
12. Comparative deletions: "a better way, faster results" with the comparison deleted.
13. Pacing current experience: state 3 verifiable truths, then lead. "You're reading this page, you run a coaching business, you've tried ads, and that's exactly why this will make sense."
14. Double binds: two choices, same outcome. "Watch the demo now or book the call directly, either way you'll see the system."
15. Conversational postulate: a question that acts as a command. "Can you picture your calendar full?"
16. Extended quotes: nest the claim inside a story someone else said. Deniable proof.
17. Selection restriction violation: give objects feelings. "Your calendar is begging for this."
18. Embedded commands: hide imperatives inside sentences and mark them (bold, italics, line breaks in written copy). "When people like you join the program they get results fast."
19. Embedded questions: "I'm curious whether you can see the value already."
20. Covering all possibilities: "Pay in full, split it, or start with the pilot, whichever fits."
21. Utilization: take any objection and make it the reason to buy. "(I'm not sold.) Of course you're not, I haven't shown you the one thing that matters yet."
22. Context and meaning reframing: see section 9.
23. Building excitement: "In a few minutes I'll show you how to double your show rate with zero extra spend."
24. Truisms about sensations and time: undeniable statements that open the door. "Sooner or later every founder realizes ads are math."
25. Open-ended suggestions: "There's no limit to what a dialed-in offer can do."
26. Single binds: the more X, the more Y. "The longer you wait, the more calls you're leaving on the table."
27. "I'm not going to tell you...": asserts while disclaiming. "I'm not going to tell you this is the only system that works. I'll let the numbers do that."
28. Compound suggestions: suggestion + undeniable fact. "Join the pilot. Getting clients matters, doesn't it?"
29. Phonological ambiguity: sound-alike words (by, buy, know, no). Use sparingly in hooks.
30. Syntactic ambiguity: "Selling coaches can be tricky."
31. Scope ambiguity: modifier applies to more than one thing. Use rarely.
32. Punctuation ambiguity: two statements welded together. Spoken VSL only, not written copy.

## 8. Sleight of Mouth: 18 Ways to Reframe Any Objection

Objections arrive as A means B, A causes B, or A equals B. Rephrase into that form, then pick a pattern. Example objection: "Your price is too high" means "I can't afford it."

1. Meta-frame: "You're only saying that because you haven't seen what it returns."
2. Change frame size: chunk up to a universal. "You only get what you pay for."
3. Apply A to itself: "Thinking that is a high price to pay."
4. Apply B to itself: "You can't afford NOT to afford it."
5. Reality strategy: "How do you tell the difference between price and value?"
6. Model of the world: "Has buying cheap ever actually satisfied you?"
7. Intent: "So your real goal is to protect cash flow. Good, that's what this does."
8. Redefine A: "Price is not value. Price is the entry cost, tiny next to the return."
9. Redefine B: "Affording isn't the issue, allocation is."
10. Counter example: "Ever bought something cheap that was terrible value?"
11. Chunk up: "What matters more, the fee or what the fee buys?"
12. Chunk down: "What price would you put on 10 booked calls a week?"
13. Metaphor: tell the story of the cheap tool that cost more in rework.
14. Another outcome: "Whether the price is high isn't the issue. Whether it pays for itself is."
15. Consequences: "Keep thinking that way and every decision becomes a cost decision, never an investment decision."
16. Hierarchy of criteria: "Isn't growing the business what's really important here?"
17. Take it to threshold: stretch it to absurdity. "Some people would pay ten times this for the outcome."
18. Reversing presuppositions: "In what way could the price actually mean you can't afford to wait?"

FAQ sections, objection blocks in VSLs, sales scripts, and email sequences should each pick 3 to 5 of these per objection, never all 18.

## 9. The Two Reframes

- Meaning reframe: same event, new meaning. Trigger: "A means B" complaints. Ask "what else could this behavior mean, in what larger frame is it valuable?" Statement: "Keeping the same team means we can't transform." Reframe: "Your team is the critical mass that makes the change stick."
- Context reframe: same behavior, new context where it's valuable. Trigger: "I am too X, he is too X." Statement: "He's too stubborn." Reframe: "That's exactly the person you want holding a project until it's done."
- Reframes don't force action. They only work if the new frame is undeniably valid AND makes more sense than the old one. Fake reframes read as spin.

## 10. Cartesian Logic: The 4-Question Close

- What would happen if you did?
- What would happen if you didn't?
- What wouldn't happen if you did?
- What wouldn't happen if you didn't?

Use in VSL closes, sales scripts, and cart-close emails. The four together force the prospect to represent the full decision space, including the cost of inaction twice.

## 11. Chaining Modal Operators

Walk necessity down into possibility: can't, shouldn't have to, could, can, will. "I know you think you can't do this. Perhaps you shouldn't have to. But could you realize it's something you CAN do? You don't have to. Should you want to, you'll find it's possible." Use in nurture emails for skeptical lists.

## 12. Amplifiers and Devices for Written Copy

- Semantically charged words: totally, honestly, fully, intensely, completely, absolutely, utterly, powerfully, driven. Sprinkle to raise intensity, never stack three in a sentence.
- Stacking representations: build wanted pictures one on top of another (money, time, respect) then tag-question it: "That feels right, doesn't it?"
- Elicitation of universal experiences: anchor to things everyone has lived: shopping for something you just HAD to have, Christmas-morning curiosity, learning to ride a bike, a belief you once held that turned out false. Perfect openers for emails and VSL leads.
- Time-released suggestions: plant a belief early ("the best operators ask questions"), fulfill it later ("you've read this far, which tells me which kind you are"). Set up in the lead, pay off at the CTA.
- Quotes as experts: "People who understand acquisition say you can't out-write a broken offer." Authority without a citation fight. Never fabricate a specific named expert or stat: use [PROOF:] placeholders for real ones.
- The STOP pattern: "Stop and picture...", "Stop and imagine...", "If you stop and think about...". Interrupts internal processing and opens a slot for the suggestion that follows. One per page maximum.
- Future pacing: "Imagine it's a year from now. What did you have to do to get here?" Put them in the after-state, then have them look back at buying as the obvious step.
- Misc high-value templates: "Don't (buy) unless you really want to (benefit)." / "(Phrase) because (phrase), therefore (benefit)." / "You may not have thought you could afford this in the past, but at this price you cannot not consider it." / "No matter how hard you try, you can't ignore..." / "Just because X doesn't mean Y." / "When you have already decided that now is the time, book the call."

## 13. The 56 Writing and Brochure Templates (The Crown Jewel)

McLaughlin's fill-in-the-blank persuasion sentences for written marketing. The strongest 30, generalized:

1. "Please (something they'd do anyway) before you (what I want): Please read this entire letter before you book your spot." Presupposes the booking.
2. "Maybe it's not (small negative). But (powerful claim): Maybe it's not the easiest thing you'll do. But you'll learn the most effective client system ever taught."
3. Not-yet desire builder: "In a moment I'll tell you what others have paid thousands to learn."
4. Price anchor: "Many people have paid thousands for this. You can learn it for an unbelievably low cost."
5. Negative-to-positive recall: "Recall your most frustrating month of chasing clients. Was it last quarter? Now imagine that same month, but with this system running."
6. "When (you do what I want) you'll find yourself quickly and easily (becoming what you want to be)."
7. "(What I want you to do) is an easily learned skill."
8. Qualifier IN: "If your week includes talking to prospects, clients, or a team, this is for you."
9. Qualifier OUT (takeaway): "So if you're looking for a magic button, this is not for you."
10. "When (action), (small bonus): When you book, mention this page and you'll get the onboarding call free."
11. "I don't know about you but I (embedded command) when..."
12. "Maybe I'm not (minor flaw). But I do know (powerful claim)."
13. "In a moment I'll tell you (the thing you want interest in)."
14. Observed-interest opener for outreach: "I've seen your interest in (topic) from (evidence). And I know you have a desire to (what the offer does)."
15. "As you (take the action), you'll truly (benefit)."
16. Seriousness frame: "So the question is: are you serious about (the outcome)?"
17. Scarcity of attention: "You can bring your doubts, but there's no guarantee you'll use them. Expect to be busy (getting results)."
18. Attitude priming: "Realize (founder) is dead serious about your results and requires the same from you."
19. Social contact value: "Many people say they get as much from the onboarding call as from the whole program."
20. "And boy did (person they know) get excited when..."
21. Scarcity aside: "Here's the bonus. (Which I seriously doubt will ever be repeated.)"
22. Concession close: "Pay in full at $X. Or Larry-style: split it, and of course if you split it, you give up the free bonus." People hate giving up a FREE BONUS. Exclamation marks signal excitement to the skimming eye, use one or two, not ten.
23. Bonus-on-action: "As soon as you book, you'll get (asset). Just fill it in and send it back. When you arrive, your plan will be waiting."
24. Particle proof: "Once you've used even one piece of this, you'll swear by it."
25. Credibility transfer: "(Founder)'s thousands of hours studying (field) taught them (the mechanism)."
26. "(Statement). Even when (opposite)!" Sounds logical regardless of content, use with true statements only.
27. 99% frame: "(Claim). That's where 99% of us go wrong. The truth is (what you want believed)."
28. "The bottom line is (the time, money, and frustration it saves, day in and day out)."
29. Belief transfer: "That's how strongly I believe in this. And that's how much I know you'll value it too."
30. One-piece value math: "So simply do this: watch the demo. Then ask yourself, isn't it possible that just one of these systems is worth more than the whole fee?"
31. Above-the-herd presupposition: "Don't get caught in moo-cow thinking. You clearly have a desire to break your own limits. Of course that's unusual. Most people are content being one of the herd." Only the above-average buy, and everyone believes they're above average.
32. "I'm absolutely convinced you've already made that step and broken from the herd."
33. Post-purchase future pace: "When you leave Sunday night, walking on air, saying to yourself: My God, I can do this. Look out world."
34. "I'm going to show you (mechanism) in such a way that you'll be able to (outcome), whether you want to or not."

## 14. Embedded Commands: The Written Version

- In writing, mark commands with bold, italics, capitals, or line position, the eye registers marked text the way the ear registers tonal shifts.
- Command bank: really look forward to, listen to this, accomplish your goals, agree with me, you want to learn, see this clearly, believe what I say, you should enroll, get excited, think about, see yourself, want to come, achieve your dreams, make changes, register now, reserve a spot, don't be left out.
- The book's own demo brochure stacks: "Please read this entire notice before you..." + "WARNING!!" + scarcity ("there may not be enough room for everyone") + quoted urgency ("People are already saying: Don't wait, register now!") + "I don't know about you but I get excited when..." + "Maybe it's not going to cost you thousands. But you'll practice and learn the most powerful techniques..." + "You can't afford not to attend! Can You?" + "This is a one time offer made only to people like yourself, so plan to attend."
- Density rule: that demo is a maximum-density showcase. In real funnel copy run 1 embedded command per 2 to 3 paragraphs, or the copy reads hypey and dies at the third-grade-reading-level check.

## 15. The Meta Model: The Research Tool (Reverse of Everything Above)

The Milton Model goes abstract to persuade. The Meta Model goes specific to recover deleted information. Use it on MARKET RESEARCH and CLIENT ONBOARDING, not in copy.

- Distortions: mind reading ("how do you know?"), lost performative ("who says?"), cause-effect ("how specifically does X cause Y?"), complex equivalence ("how does X mean Y?").
- Generalizations: universal quantifiers ("never? what would happen if you did?"), modal operators ("what stops you? what would happen if you did?").
- Deletions: nominalizations ("who's deciding? decided what?"), unspecified verbs ("how specifically?"), comparative deletions ("better than what?"), unspecified nouns ("who exactly?").
- Extras: either-or phrases ("is there an in-between?"), static words ("what science, says what?"), characterizations ("what do they actually do?"), predictions ("how can you know the future?").
- When mining reviews, forums, and sales calls for the avatar doc, every vague complaint hides a specific one. "Coaches never respect my time" becomes the specific scene copy needs: the 9pm Slack message, the no-show call. Chase the deletion until you hit a scene you can picture.

## 16. Where This Maps in Our Pipeline

- Market research and avatar docs: use the Meta Model (section 15) to convert vague market language into concrete scenes. Note which logical level (section 6) the market's complaints live at.
- Foundation docs and offer docs: presupposition word banks (section 3) in the offer naming: proven, lasting, automatic. Cartesian logic (section 10) to stress-test the offer's cost of inaction.
- VSL and webinar scripts: pacing current experience (7.13) in the lead, universal experiences (12) as openers, verb tense shifts (2) throughout, future pacing plus excitement (12, 13.33) in the close, Cartesian logic in the final ask. Punctuation and phonological ambiguity are allowed here since it's spoken.
- Funnel copy and landing pages: the 56 templates (13) are the swipe layer, qualifier in and out (13.8, 13.9), takeaway selling, one-piece value math (13.30), concession close (13.22). Embedded commands marked with bold, at low density (14). End every section on the wanted representation (1).
- Ads and hooks: rhetorical questions, "I'm not going to tell you", the STOP pattern, single binds ("the longer you wait..."). Comparative deletions for curiosity ("a faster way to get clients") as long as the mechanism is revealed in the funnel.
- Emails: chaining modal operators (11) for cold and skeptical lists, time-released suggestions (12) across a sequence: plant in email 1, pay off in email 4, double binds in the CTA line.
- Sales scripts and AI voice agent: Sleight of Mouth (8) is the objection layer, pick 3 to 5 patterns per objection and script them. Tag questions and conversational postulates to move to booking. Utilization (7.21) for any curveball.
- Client onboarding: Meta Model questions to pull specifics out of the client's vague answers, exactly like SPIN's investigating stage.
- Copy QA: check negation (does any line install the wrong picture?), check tense (is the problem in the past and result in the present?), check sentence-final position (does each section end on the wanted state?), check embedded-command density, check that every reframe is undeniably valid, not spin.
