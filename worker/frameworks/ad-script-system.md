# Ad Script System: Full-Length DR Video Ads (Skill Bundle Distillation)

Source: ad-script-writer skill bundle (SKILL.md + hook-bank.md + script-formats.md + diversification-engine.md). This is the production system for paid Meta video ads (talking head, UGC, voiceover) driving to a webinar, VSL, call, or DM.

## The Two Laws

1. One ad, one angle. Hook, body, and CTA all serve a single central angle for a single sub-avatar. The test: if you could swap this ad's hook onto another ad's body and it still flowed, the two ads are not differentiated enough.
2. Real proof only. Named people, real numbers, real timeframes. Never fabricate a result, a student, or scarcity. Missing proof gets flagged with [PROOF:], never invented.

You are a marketer, not a copywriter: every line is a targeting and conversion decision, not a flourish.

## Required Context Before Writing

1. The offer: what's sold, the transformation, price, funnel destination.
2. The ICP sliced into 2 to 4 sub-avatars. Each ad targets ONE.
3. The proof: named results, real numbers, real timeframes.
4. The mechanism: the named, specific reason this works when other things didn't.
5. The voice: the client's register, pulled from their content.
6. Awareness level of the traffic (decides how much runway the hook needs).
7. Optional but strong: winning competitor ads and the client's own organic winners. A concept proven organically usually wins on paid. Adapt the proven shape, never copy the words, never import a claim the client cannot prove.

If the mechanism or real proof is missing, flag it. Don't invent.

## The Diversification Engine (Angle Generation, Before Any Hook)

Meta's Andromeda delivery treats ads with the same creator, setting, and roughly 80% of the same script as ONE ad and starves the near-duplicates. Hook-splice variations no longer count. The platform rewards genuinely different full-length ads: different psychological conversations with different humans.

Step 1: define 2 to 4 sub-avatars. Same offer, meaningfully different humans. Example (body-comp coaching for busy men): used to be in shape and wants it back / tried multiple programs, never saw results / skinny-fat, fine on paper, hates how he looks / travels constantly, can't follow a routine.

Step 2: for each avatar, list buying reasons across all TEN categories (aim 30+ total before stopping):
1. Functional: what the offer does. Anchor proof here, don't lead with it.
2. Emotional: how it makes them feel underneath the outcome. The sharpest name a feeling they've had but never said aloud ("stop hating photos of yourself").
3. Social: how people who already know them perceive them after.
4. Financial: money earned, saved, or no longer wasted. For B2C transformation offers, lead with the graveyard of stuff they bought and never used.
5. Time: actual hours and mental load given back, not "more efficient."
6. Risk-mitigation: the disaster avoided. Underused, name a real risk honestly.
7. Status: being seen differently in rooms they WANT to walk into.
8. Identity: who they get to become or get back to. The deepest category. Write it as a sentence about themselves they recognize.
9. Situational: the life moment making it urgent now (milestone birthday, wedding, post-divorce, holiday photos). Under-reaches, over-converts.
10. Comparative: why this beats the category they've tried ("generic apps"), never a named competitor.

Step 3: each reason becomes one FULL ad (hook, body, CTA aligned to that one reason). For a batch: generate 10 to 30 reasons, write the strongest 12 to 20 as full ads.

Angle multipliers: perspective shift (customer, industry, competitive, future, past), stakeholder variation (themselves, spouse, kids, younger self, future self), timeline diversification (immediate to legacy), emotional-state targeting (the same prospect on a frustrated day, ambitious day, fearful day, excited day, confident day). Emotional-state targeting is the strongest door into B2C info audiences.

Messaging mix: run SPECIFIC ads (sharp, narrow, ultra-relatable, convert hard) and HERO ads (broad mass appeal, reach the TAM) in the SAME ad set of 20 to 30 creatives. Meta often serves 2 or 3 ads before conversion: hero warms, specific closes.

Format diversity is the second axis: 10 angles all filmed direct-to-camera in the same room still read as one ad. A batch covers 5+ production formats (direct-to-camera, whiteboard, podcast, green-screen, demonstration, b-roll, UGC, comment-pin, walking). Post formats organically first with no CTA, then put paid budget behind whatever pops.

Proportion note: none of this matters if the ads aren't good. When starting on paid, finding the ONE winning angle matters more than spreading wide. Write good ads first.

## The Hook Bank (First 2 to 3 Seconds)

A good hook makes them feel you're talking directly to them, says something they didn't know but want to, challenges a held belief, or shows a result they want. Overriding rule: never open with "If you're an agency owner." Use the exact phrase that group recognizes about themselves. Specificity is the whole game.

Five emotional categories with templates:

Pain (they're stuck, tried things that failed):
- "If you've been trying to [result] and keep getting [obstacle], it's not your fault."
- "Every month you wait is another month your competitor is [winning] while you're stuck."
- "Stop doing [common thing]. It's actually making your [problem] worse."

Desire (they need inspiring):
- "What would it look like if you could [dream outcome] without [biggest obstacle]?"
- "Copy my [system] and get [result] in [timeframe]."

Proof (only when names and numbers are real):
- "[Name] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]. Here's exactly what we did."
- "[Number] people have used this to get [result]. Here's how it works."

Curiosity (a common mistake or something they should know):
- "There's one thing almost every [audience] does when they [situation], and it's the exact reason most of them [bad result]."
- "[Number] things about [niche] I wish someone had told me earlier."

Controversial (shake a belief, drive engagement):
- "Unpopular opinion: [common thing] is actually a waste of [time or money]."
- "[Common belief] is the biggest lie in [niche]. Here's the truth."

Eight format archetypes (how the hook is built):
1. Proof: lead with evidence. Every number appears as a text overlay, must work on silent.
2. Disruption: challenge a belief, name the cost, bridge to the better frame in the next breath.
3. Identification: speak to who they are. A category is not a person, write to a real individual.
4. Demonstration: the visual IS the hook (prop, whiteboard, location). If a talking head could replace the visual without losing anything, it's not a demonstration hook.
5. Curiosity: withhold the how, tease a NAMED mechanism. "The secret strategy" is not a mechanism.
6. Sourced / B-roll: open on a 5 to 15 second clip of a credible figure in the ICP's world, then cut to your footage. The most underused format relative to performance.
7. Emotional state: name the feeling they're having right now, ultra-specifically. "If you're frustrated" doesn't land. "If you're frustrated that every gym program you've tried was built for 25-year-olds" lands.
8. Question / Challenge: an honest, dissonance-creating question. Rhetorical reads as marketing.

Hook rules: land the one angle in the first 1 to 3 lines. Statements beat questions (questions attract skeptics). The in-market buyer is roughly 2 to 3% of the audience, write so the wrong people self-select out. Specific beats vague. The hook must bridge straight into the body.

## The Five Script Formats (Body Structure)

Pick one per ad by where the audience is:

| Format | Lead with | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| Pain | A specific frustration | Stuck, frustrated, tried things that failed |
| Desire | The dream outcome | Needs inspiring, doesn't feel the problem yet |
| Proof | A real result | You have a real win with name + number + timeframe |
| Curiosity | An open loop or contrarian gap | Common mistake or unknown they should know |
| Controversial | A bold stance | Want engagement, shake a held belief |

Pain: Hook (name a pain so specific they think you're talking to them) > Agitate (the daily price of staying stuck) > Reframe (take the blame off them: the system, the bad advice) > Solution (the offer fixes that exact pain, one solution not a feature list) > CTA (one action, low pressure, tell them what they get).

Desire: Hook (open on the dream, vivid) > Vision build (let them sit in the possibility) > Credibility bridge (real people already got this) > Mechanism (name the method briefly) > CTA (framed as moving toward the dream).

Proof: Hook (start with the result and a number, not the story) > Before state (relatable, "that's me") > The turn (the specific thing that changed everything, showing the offer is the reason) > The result (stack wins: revenue, time, lifestyle) > CTA (access to the same thing this person used).

Curiosity: Hook (imply they're missing something) > Open the loop (the weight of not knowing) > Partial reveal (teach enough to feel learning) > Close the loop (deliver the real insight, this earns trust) > CTA (more insights like this).

Controversial: Hook (bold claim immediately) > Stake your claim (double down before explaining) > The argument (logic, data, contrarian insight) > The real solution (pivot to what works) > CTA (invite engagement, give it somewhere to land).

Cross-format rules: one angle per ad, body deepens the hook's angle and never introduces a competing one. Every proof beat needs a real name, number, timeframe. Name the mechanism, don't tutorial it (the funnel does the full teach). 30 to 90 seconds spoken, about 60 ideal. Read aloud: if you stumble, the viewer will.

## Body and CTA Construction

Body flow: current situation in the viewer's own rough words > ideal situation (the congruent opposite) > proof (name, number, timeframe) > mechanism named in 1 or 2 lines. Sprinkle secondary pains to SUPPORT the central angle, never let a second angle fight the hook.

CTA: exactly what to click and what happens next. Not "learn more" but "click below, watch the short training, book a free call." Calm and confident for sophisticated buyers. Only real urgency, never a fake timer.

Final passes: dimensionalization (place every abstract line in the viewer's actual day) and humanizer (contractions throughout, no AI cadence, read-aloud test).

## Batch Verification Checklist

- Hook-swap test: no hook flows naturally onto another ad's body.
- At least 3 meaningfully different sub-avatars.
- At least 5 hook categories used.
- At least 5 production formats.
- At least 10 distinct buying reasons across the ten categories.
- Visual variation matches messaging variation.
- Each ad has one central angle.
- Read-aloud test passes on every script.

## Where This Maps in Our Pipeline

- Ad batches for clients: this file IS the process. Diversification engine first (angles from the avatar doc's sub-avatars), then format, then hook, then body. Never deliver hook variations as a batch.
- Avatar and research docs: structure market research around the 10 buying-reason categories so ad angles fall out of the research automatically. Capture the market's exact self-descriptions for identification hooks.
- Hook bank for the app's hook generator: the 5 emotional categories x 8 format archetypes grid is the generation matrix.
- VSL and landing page leads: the same hook rules apply to headlines (statements over questions, in-market language, specificity).
- Broll and static ads: emotional-state targeting and the demonstration archetype guide the visual brief.
- Copy QA: run the batch verification checklist before any ad deliverable ships. Flag missing proof with [PROOF:], never fill it.
