# The Email Machine: Delivered, Opened, Clicked (Sell Like Crazy Phase 8, mined line by line)

emails-and-booking.md governs WHAT the sequence says (arc, angles, booking
mechanics). This file governs HOW every individual email is built so it
lands, gets opened, and gets clicked. Apply to every email we ship.

## Step 1: Get it delivered

- Email = the highest-ROI channel (~$44 back per $1) and the only audience
  we OWN; rented platforms can vanish overnight.
- Sender score is the asset: engaged opens raise it, ignored mail kills it.
- Sender name is a PERSON, never sales@ or a brand-only name.
- Plain, personal-looking emails. No logos in the header (logo lives in the
  signature), no heavy HTML design, no image walls. HubSpot's own research:
  HTML-enhanced emails DECREASE opens and clicks. Plain text looks like a
  friend; designed emails scream mass marketing and get sorted to the "C"
  group and deleted.
- Ratio: roughly 2/3 pure value sends to 1/3 promotional, and even value
  emails can carry the offer quietly in the P.S.

## Step 2: Get it opened (the P group doctrine)

Inboxes sort into two buckets: P group (personal: friends, colleagues,
must-action) and C group (commercial: skimmed, deleted). Everything about
the email's envelope must say P group, without trickery or gimmicks.

Subject lines:
- Look personal: lowercase or sentence case, almost no punctuation, no Title
  Case, never ALL CAPS
- FOUR TO FIVE WORDS MAX for the personal style ("crazy check this out",
  "approval needed", "can I chat to you about this?")
- Ask questions; tell enough to open, never the whole story
- Length data: 1-20 characters = highest open (18.5%), click (2.4%), and
  click-to-open (12.9%) rates. 41-50 characters is the DEATH ZONE (the
  average marketer's length; worst performance). Rule: two-to-four words OR
  super long, nothing in between.
- Clickbait-style intrigue is allowed ONLY if the email body closes the loop
  it opens; unclosed loops teach readers to stop opening (email blindness).
- Formulaic subjects ("7 secrets to...") fatigue fast; use the swipe formulas
  as a last resort, not a default.

Preheader text (the sniff test):
- The second thing they see; write it deliberately, never let the client
  default to "view in browser"
- Burning with intrigue, never the whole story: take a mid-sentence slice of
  body copy, or a cliff-hanger/open loop ("I walked into my manager's office
  and said these 3 simple words...")
- Cliff-hanger stems that always work: "Let me explain", "Here's what I
  mean", "Sound silly? It's not", "Case in point", "This is how"

Assess the terrain: know what the reader's inbox looks like (competitors'
sends) and do the OPPOSITE: they go long, we go short; they design, we
write plain.

Timing defaults (test against the list): Tuesday 10am is king; then
Thursday 8pm, Wednesday 2pm, Tuesday 6am. Evening sends (8pm-midnight)
outperform expectations.

## Step 3: Get it clicked

Anatomy: Subject/Preheader -> Lead -> Body -> CTA. One slippery slope: each
element's only job is to pull them to the next line.

- **The #1 rule: be entertaining.** Nobody is ever bored into buying.
  Readers are two clicks from infinite entertainment; a dry email loses to
  a cat video every time. Passion, showmanship, personality, polarising
  takes. Never lie, never trick, never insult; but never water down.
- **Tone**: write like a friend. "Hey there" openers are fine. Short
  sentences. Single-line paragraphs. Personal stories and banter FIRST,
  then the valuable idea. Read it aloud: if it doesn't sound like talking
  to a best friend, rewrite.
- **It's about them**: glimpses of your life are the seasoning; what you
  can do for THEM is the meal.
- **Visceral specificity**: vague copy causes email blindness. "Increase
  your sales" is dead. "Imagine what it would feel like to double your
  sales in the next 90 days... you'd finally switch off on weekends" lives.
  Every claim gets painted as a scene in their life with numbers.
- **Embedded-command CTAs**: don't ask, TELL. "Go ahead and check this out
  now." "Claim your spot here." "Click here and I'll tell you what it's all
  about." One command, one destination, restated in the P.S.

## The Breakthrough Email Checklist (run on every email)

1. Personal sender name
2. Subject: 2-4 words or super long; personal-looking; never the death zone
3. Preheader burns with intrigue
4. Entertains, excites, engages (the boredom test)
5. "Will this land in the P group or die in the C group?"
6. Plain text, no design chrome
7. Best-friend tone, about the reader
8. Opposite of what the herd's inbox looks like
9. Visceral specifics, no vague claims
10. Commands, not requests; loop opened by the subject gets closed

## Where this maps in our pipeline

- **Email sequences deliverable**: every email in the 14-day arc passes the
  checklist; subjects rewritten to P-group style; preheaders written
  explicitly for all 14.
- **Skool posts/DMs**: same P-group doctrine applies to DM scripts and post
  titles (lowercase, short, personal, curiosity).
- **Client onboarding docs**: sender-name and plain-text rules go into the
  email platform setup instructions.
