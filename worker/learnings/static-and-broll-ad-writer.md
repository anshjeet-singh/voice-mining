# Learnings: static-and-broll-ad-writer

Accumulated craft lessons applied on every run.

<!-- 2026-07-10 · job 180009 · client: Trent Kus -->
- The bundled native-formats.md says a brand header fails the native test, but does not state the corollary plainly: phone-native formats (iMessage, Notes, search, tweet, Reminders, lock screen) carry NO brand chrome at all, and brand colours/logos belong only to the direct-response family (comparison, stat slam, marker maths, workshop, etc.). A one-line rule in the craft section would stop the recurring mistake of stamping a logo on a "screenshot" ad.
- Note that in regulated-category niches, the "state the pricing model, never the number" rule extends to on-image proof: a named client dollar result should not be banked as a bare on-image number in a format that cannot show its screenshot (e.g. a search snippet). Point the format at the receipt instead.

<!-- 2026-07-10 · job 210001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- The red-lines and craft-rules in references/native-formats.md should call out that a client's no-em-dash rule applies to rendered UI text, and name the sneaky slot: search-result titles, email subject lines, and tweet display text where a dash reads as "natural chrome." A comma or colon holds the format without breaking the client rule.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 240001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Every ad's copy must self-identify its market and its avatar so both Meta and the scrolling viewer instantly know what the ad is and who it targets. A hook that could sit on any niche's ad ("show me a check," "I doubled my income") is a fail, even when it is a real customer verbatim: name the niche and the situation in the first line. This was an operator rejection ("every ad needs to be able to tell Meta exactly what the ad is about and who it is targeting"), and it holds for every client, not just this one.
- On a text-only manifesto or occupation-callout static, less is more: one hero idea, one hero word, generous spacing. Operators read a cramped seven-line block of equal-weight text as weak execution even when the copy is right. Cut to the "if you're a ___ and want ___" skeleton and let the whitespace carry it.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 240001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Every native static must ship with a working call-to-action in the format's native slot (the final blue iMessage bubble, the marker line under a Notes card, the last caption of a b-roll). The stance that "an honest CTA-free native beats a broken one" was rejected outright by the operator: a static with no CTA reads to him as burning money. If a native format has no obvious CTA slot, engineer one (a final link-styled line) rather than shipping CTA-free and leaning on the primary text.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- In a regulated niche (funding, credit, finance, health), a hard quantified result with a specific timeline (a "550 to 790 in two weeks" score jump) is not a design problem, it is an angle problem. It reads like the exact scams a trust-led batch warns against and it trips a platform financial-services review, so it poisons the whole set. Reject the angle rather than redesign it, and carry the proof in a native recreation instead.
- Drop protest-too-much language. Repeating "real" (real client, real disputes, real before/after) three or four times in one ad, or pinning a tiny "results vary" line directly under a giant claim, signals a scam to a burned audience. Make the claim cleanly or drop it; do not try to have it both ways.
- Replace weak, checkable proof ("zero bad reviews online") with a concrete capability stat the prospect cannot easily disprove ("over 100 owners funded or repaired"). Inviting the audience to go verify a soft claim usually backfires.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270002 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Match the CTA's required awareness to the ad's awareness level. A "look me up / verify me / search my name" ask only converts solution-aware traffic that already has a reason to research the founder. On a cold, problem-aware static it dies, because a problem-aware viewer will not go do homework. For problem-aware placements, make the ask a low-friction next step (join the free community, see how it works), and save due-diligence asks for warmer, solution-aware surfaces.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270002 · client: Trent Kus -->
- On a problem-aware cold ad, never make "look me up" or "verify me" the ask. A prospect who has never heard of the brand will not spend time on due diligence; that ask only works on solution-aware traffic. Problem-aware statics must route to a low-friction educational next step (a free community, a short training), not a research task.
- Fee and cost-objection statics win on brevity. The shortest possible framing of the pay-on-results structure ("Small upfront, we only get paid once you get funded") beats a longer worked example. Lead with the structure, cut the math.
- The "answer engine" native format (ChatGPT, Claude, AI chat) should lead its answer body with the CTA line, not bury the takeaway. Frame the operator as the worked example inside the answer ("X is a great example of someone who does this, click the link below to see how") rather than a passive "the tell is..." close.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270013 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Every ad ends with the batch's standard CTA line in the format's native slot. A creative that just stops, with no CTA while every other ad in the batch carries the link line, fails.
- Avoid generic stock visual pairings (anchor plus rocket, "old way vs new way" split panels). Combined with a template layout they read as a stock Canva file with the client's words dropped in.
- Do not put unexplained insider jargon on the most important element of a cold ad ("THE MILLS" on the key data point). Cold prospects do not know the category's internal terms; confusion at the moment of the pitch kills it. Spell it plainly.
- Every ad must serve the batch's governing angle for its target avatar. An ad that lectures a different person (debt-responsibility copy aimed at a can't-get-approved audience) is an angle failure, not a design fix. Reject the off-angle concept rather than reskin it.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270015 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Never put unexplained insider jargon on the most important element of a cold ad. A cold prospect does not know that credit-sweep companies are called "the mills," so labelling the key data point "THE MILLS" creates confusion at the exact moment of the pitch. Name the enemy in plain words the cold viewer already uses.
- A mechanism ad must OPEN the curiosity loop, not close it. Spelling out the entire method flat ("the whole method in three moves: position, sequence, place") leaves "see how it works" offering the prospect nothing they do not already have. Show that a method exists and that it flips the outcome; make the click the way to get the method.
- Match the emotional target to what the audience actually believes. For a burned, repeatedly-denied audience the barrier is "it's rigged / it's a scam," not "it's complicated," so "it's simpler than you think" aims at the wrong objection. Read the belief before choosing the reassurance.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270015 · client: Trent Kus -->
- A mechanism static that names every step of the method ("here's the whole method in three moves") closes the curiosity loop the ad exists to open. Once the prospect has read the method, the "see how it works" CTA offers them nothing they do not already have. Mechanism statics must OPEN the loop (name that a specific order, step, or "one thing" exists) and hand the reveal to the funnel, never teach all the steps on the image.
- Unexplained insider jargon on the ad's single most important data point (industry labels the audience uses internally, e.g. "the mills," "the sweep crowd") confuses cold prospects at the exact moment of the pitch. Name the enemy by its behaviour ("charges thousands up front, then goes quiet," "wants it all up front") not by a label the cold prospect has never heard. A jargon label is only safe when a plain-language contrast sits directly beside it.
- Match the emotional target to what the audience actually believes. For a burned, repeatedly-denied buyer the feeling is "it's rigged," not "it's complicated," so a "simpler than you think" promise aims at the wrong emotion. Lead with the belief they already hold, then reframe it.

<!-- 2026-07-13 · job 360005 · client: Blake Matthews -->
- A search/SERP clone must resolve the problem into the branded solution, not stack problem snippets. Mirror the reference's structure of two costly or painful options (that get flagged, that hold your funds) collapsing into one smart branded result that names the solution and sells the free click, then a bold kicker. A SERP that only lists problems and ends on a soft kicker does not sell the click.
- A free-community-join native ad must make the niche legible on the image and name the free community with the join CTA in the format's own slot. A proof story that never says what the business is, or never names the free room, fails even when the numbers are real: the viewer cannot tell what he is joining or why.

<!-- 2026-07-13 · job 360005 · client: Blake Matthews -->
- A big-text / proclamation static must anchor its copy to the specific product or income mechanism, never a generic status slogan. An operator rejected a "500k a month is the new 100k" capped-earner poster with "not sure how this even relates to selling [the product]." Proclamation formats still need at least one concrete line naming where the money comes from.
- Cover every distinct low-friction avatar in the batch. When the ICP splits into a high-ticket buyer and a low-capital consumer/affiliate avatar, ship at least one static on the affiliate/consumer income angle (make $X a month, no product, no capital). Operators notice and reject when a whole avatar is left unexplored.
- On an "option 1 vs option 2" comparison or napkin static, write the winning column as the dream outcomes the buyer wants, not as steps or homework. "Learn the compliance line first" was rejected for not selling the outcome; lead option 2 with the payoff (what running it actually gets you), then let the funnel teach.

<!-- 2026-07-13 · job 360006 · client: Blake Matthews -->
- System-wide targeting rule: never write copy that signals "broke" or "no capital / no money to start." That language attracts unqualified leads. Before writing a hook, ask "what kind of buyer does this attract?" Reframe the same low-friction offer around the outcome and the avoidance of hassle (e.g. "the best way to make $10k a month without starting a business") instead of the absence of money. This holds for every client, not just this one.
- Never render an assumed or unconfirmed brand/community/product name (or a made-up domain) onto ad creative. If the name is not confirmed by the client, route generically (e.g. "the free community", the real platform URL like skool.com, "click the link below") and leave the specific name/domain as a token for the client to supply.
