# Learnings: static-and-broll-ad-writer

Accumulated craft lessons applied on every run.

<!-- 2026-07-10 · job 180009 · client: Trent Kus -->
- The bundled native-formats.md says a brand header fails the native test, but does not state the corollary plainly: phone-native formats (iMessage, Notes, search, tweet, Reminders, lock screen) carry NO brand chrome at all, and brand colours/logos belong only to the direct-response family (comparison, stat slam, marker maths, workshop, etc.). A one-line rule in the craft section would stop the recurring mistake of stamping a logo on a "screenshot" ad.
- Note that in regulated-category niches, the "state the pricing model, never the number" rule extends to on-image proof: a named client dollar result should not be banked as a bare on-image number in a format that cannot show its screenshot (e.g. a search snippet). Point the format at the receipt instead.

<!-- 2026-07-10 · job 210001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- The red-lines and craft-rules in references/native-formats.md should call out that a client's no-em-dash rule applies to rendered UI text, and name the sneaky slot: search-result titles, email subject lines, and tweet display text where a dash reads as "natural chrome." A comma or colon holds the format without breaking the client rule.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 240001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Every ad's copy must self-identify its market and its avatar so both Meta and the scrolling viewer instantly know what the ad is and who it targets. A hook that could sit on any niche's ad ("show me a check," "I doubled my income") is a fail, even when it is a real customer verbatim: name the niche and the situation in the first line. This was an operator rejection ("every ad needs to be able to tell Meta exactly what the ad is about and who it is targeting"), and it holds for every client, not just this one.
- On a text-only manifesto or occupation-callout static, less is more: one hero idea, one hero word, generous spacing. Operators read a cramped seven-line block of equal-weight text as weak execution even when the copy is right. Cut to the "if you're a ___ and want ___" skeleton and let the whitespace carry it.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 240001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Every native static must ship with a working call-to-action in the format's native slot (the final blue iMessage bubble, the marker line under a Notes card, the last caption of a b-roll). The stance that "an honest CTA-free native beats a broken one" was rejected outright by the operator: a static with no CTA reads to him as burning money. If a native format has no obvious CTA slot, engineer one (a final link-styled line) rather than shipping CTA-free and leaning on the primary text.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270001 · client: Trent Kus -->
- In a regulated niche (funding, credit, finance, health), a hard quantified result with a specific timeline (a "550 to 790 in two weeks" score jump) is not a design problem, it is an angle problem. It reads like the exact scams a trust-led batch warns against and it trips a platform financial-services review, so it poisons the whole set. Reject the angle rather than redesign it, and carry the proof in a native recreation instead.
- Drop protest-too-much language. Repeating "real" (real client, real disputes, real before/after) three or four times in one ad, or pinning a tiny "results vary" line directly under a giant claim, signals a scam to a burned audience. Make the claim cleanly or drop it; do not try to have it both ways.
- Replace weak, checkable proof ("zero bad reviews online") with a concrete capability stat the prospect cannot easily disprove ("over 100 owners funded or repaired"). Inviting the audience to go verify a soft claim usually backfires.

<!-- 2026-07-11 · job 270002 · client: Trent Kus -->
- Match the CTA's required awareness to the ad's awareness level. A "look me up / verify me / search my name" ask only converts solution-aware traffic that already has a reason to research the founder. On a cold, problem-aware static it dies, because a problem-aware viewer will not go do homework. For problem-aware placements, make the ask a low-friction next step (join the free community, see how it works), and save due-diligence asks for warmer, solution-aware surfaces.
